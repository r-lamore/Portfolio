The Problem

The problem with traditional synthesis

Our research process was sound — but it had a throughput ceiling. Gaps in market awareness were only surfacing after testing had started, and the fix was expensive every time.

01

Human reading has a ceiling

At GearLab, every research cycle starts the same way: analyze what competitors are doing, identify the top sellers, and flag anything new or notable in the market. Our team is good at this — but human reading only moves so fast, and a researcher can only absorb so many products in a day before signal degrades.

02

Missing a product late is expensive

Realizing a key product was left out of a test lineup after testing has already started means retests, rescheduled logistics, and a slowed shipping pipeline. Getting the product selection right upfront isn't just a nicety — it's the foundation everything else is built on.

03

Test plans without shared context drift

When the rationale behind a test plan lives in one researcher's head, the rest of the team is writing and evaluating in a vacuum. Downstream work — the reviews, the recommendations, the audience communication — loses coherence without a clear foundation everyone can reference.

"Getting the lineup right before testing begins is the decision that everything downstream depends on. Miss it early, and you're paying for it late."

— Rachael Lamore, Senior Research Analyst, GearLab
The Solution

Three prompts that protect research capital

Rather than automating judgment, I used AI to fill the gaps where human bandwidth was the bottleneck. Each prompt serves a distinct purpose in the pre-fieldwork, lineup, and consumer-framing stages.

Stage 01
Test plan creation
Testing Method Generator
What is the best way, in both a qualitative and quantitative fashion, to test "[product]"? Use methods that regular people will easily understand, whose outputs will be equally straightforward and relatable.

Forces the test plan to stay grounded in real consumer experience, not internal jargon. Keeps our rigorous data meaningful to a busy person skimming a review at 10pm.

Stage 02
Product lineup scoping
Lineup Optimizer
What are the most important products for a top-of-search review in this category? Evaluate this lineup — what's still current, what's outdated? Include potential award ideas and what could improve search rank and traffic: [URL]

Evaluates our current product lineup against the market — flagging what's outdated, what we're missing, and what we'd need to improve to rank higher in search. Prevents over-buying and under-buying alike.

Stage 03
Audience alignment
Consumer Decision Framework
What are the most critical factors a consumer needs to know before purchasing "[product]"? What are the key purchase decisions they're making?

Anchors the test plan to what actually drives purchase decisions — so the research we run maps directly to the questions a real buyer is asking, not just the specs that are easy to measure.

Results

AI as a decision filter, not a speed tool

The real value isn't that we move faster — it's that we stop moving in the wrong direction.

Research bandwidth is finite. The costliest mistake isn't a slow report; it's a month of work invested in a direction the data never supported. AI helps us catch that before we commit.

Scenario A
Gap caught early
Starting point
Team proposes a product lineup for a category review based on manual research and team expertise. Testing is about to begin.
AI Spotlight
The lineup optimizer surfaces a high-search-volume product that was absent from the proposed list — a newer market entrant that had climbed to top-seller status but hadn't appeared in the team's manual scan.
Outcome
Product added before purchase. Testing proceeds with a complete lineup. No retest needed, no shipping delay — the gap that would have cost weeks is caught in an afternoon.
Scenario B
Strategy realigned
Starting point
Reviews had become increasingly dense — heavy on charts, data-driven language, and technical specs. The team was proud of the rigor but noticed engagement softening.
AI Spotlight
Running the consumer decision framework against several categories surfaced a consistent theme: the factors consumers actually weigh before buying are simpler and more experiential than what we were testing and communicating. The AI independently validated what some on the team had sensed but hadn't yet named.
Outcome
The whole team got a shared reminder of the mission: objective data earns trust, but clear writing about why it matters is what actually helps a busy consumer decide. The AI didn't discover this — it gave the team language to realign around it together.
"We don't yet have a formal measurement framework for research ROI. That's a gap — and one I'd want to close in a PM role."
Honest reflection

The outcomes described above are real, but they're largely qualitative. I haven't yet built the tracking infrastructure to measure them rigorously.

What we do measure

Whether research influenced a go/no-go decision on a product. Whether stakeholders requested a follow-up study. Whether insights appeared in product briefs.

What I'd build next

A lightweight research impact tracker — tagging each study to a product decision and following up 60 days later on whether that decision held.

Implications

What I actually learned from this

The most interesting finding wasn't efficiency. It was what the AI reflected back about what we were already doing — and what we'd quietly drifted from.

I
AI often confirms what your team already knows — and that's valuable

One of the more interesting outcomes was how often the AI's output mirrored strategy the team had already landed on. I had a growing feeling that our reviews were too dense — heavy on charts and data-driven jargon that validated our rigor but wasn't actually serving a busy consumer making a decision. Running the prompts gave the whole team a shared, documented reminder of that balance: objective data earns trust, but clear writing about why it matters is what helps someone actually choose.

II
The real risk isn't too few products — it's the wrong calibration

In broad categories, the lineup decision is genuinely high-stakes. Miss a product people are searching for and you bounce them off your page. Buy too many and you bloat the testing and writing process in ways that slow everything down. The AI helps finesse that balancing act — capturing the items that need to be there and flagging the ones that don't, before a single product is purchased.

III
Shared documentation changes team dynamics

One of the quieter benefits has been the documentation itself. When a test plan is grounded in an AI-generated framework that everyone has seen, there's a shared reference point. Writers, researchers, and editors are all oriented to the same consumer purchase drivers — which means downstream work coheres in ways it didn't before, without anyone having to chase down context.

IV
The judgment layer still belongs to the humans

None of this replaces the expertise that makes GearLab's reviews worth reading. The AI surfaces what's popular, what's missing, and what consumers say they care about. The team still decides what actually matters, how to test it rigorously, and how to communicate it in a way that's genuinely useful. AI clears the path. The researchers still have to walk it.
